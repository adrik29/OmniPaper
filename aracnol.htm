HarvEx is a simple computer program which allows users to explore the database of Opiliones derived from Kury’s catalogue (WCO) through diverse queries.
With HarvEx it is possible for example to know how many valid endemic species were described by Roewer from 1912 to 1914 from what is now Serbia, and how these species are distributed in genera, subfamilies, etc.
or...
to know in which countries the family Cosmetidae is distributed and how large a slice of its diversity is taken by that country.
or....
to produce instantly a checklist of all Opiliones species from Mauritania....
Of course, for the situation in the first example above to be possible it takes painstaking research.
Imagine someone recording a species from locality “Rskvrstzenica” in Yugoslavia. It takes some digging to discover that the current name is Rzcsvrstzenica and it is located in Serbia.
And if I don’t do one by one this I may miss the record of that species from that country.
But wait.... actually this locality is currently in southern Serbia which is now a different country, Kosovo... but........ with only limited recognition by the UN members... Hm... then comes the question, should I or shouldn't I discriminate Kosovo in my catalog? and why? And Transnistria? Should I recognize every self declared country? Where should I draw the line?
Remember that not even China has 100% recognition...
This is a matter for another note, but I raised the question here just to illustrate that even not refining the distribution of species beyond "country" level can be a huge task. Older literature has incredible variety of regional denominations and can be labyrinthine.

Acceptance of states in the Harvestmen Explorer (HarvEx) and in the World Catalogue of Opiliones (WCO) - Where should the line be drawn?
Disclaimer: the acceptance of 197 sovereign states in these two projects is unrelated to the specific diplomatic position of Brazil, and follows the criteria explained below.

1) All 193 member states of the United Nations (UN) are accepted in HarvEx (where they are assigned three-letter codes from ISO 3166-1 alpha-3) and the World Catalog of Opiliones (WCO).
Even the following six states with less than 100% recognition enter the list of HarvEx, as detailed in the following remarks:
Armenia (ARM) – independent since 1991, is not recognized by one UN member, Pakistan.
Cyprus, Republic of (CYP) – independent since 1960, is not recognized by one UN member (Turkey) and one UN non-member (Northern Cyprus).
Israel (ISR) – founded in 1948, is not recognized by 31 UN members.
North Korea (PRK) – independent since 1948, is not recognized by three UN members: France, Japan, South Korea; and one non-UN member: Taiwan.
People's Republic of China (CHN) – The People's Republic of China maintains diplomatic relations with most countries in the world. In 1971, the People's Republic of China replaced the Republic of China, commonly known as "Taiwan"; since the 1970s, as the sole representative of China in the United Nations and as one of the five permanent members of the United Nations Security Council. Not recognized by non-member Taiwan.
South Korea (KOR) – independent since 1948, is not recognized by one UN member, North Korea.

2) One UN observer state not recognized by at least one UN member state is accepted in HarvEx and WCO:
State of Palestine (PSE) – declared in 1988; de jure sovereign state recognized by 137 UN member states, the Holy See, and the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic. Not recognized by Israel, USA, Canada, Mexico, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Australia, New Zealand, and most of the European Union, among others.

3) Three states that are neither UN members nor UN observers but which have expressive recognition are accepted in HarvEx and WCO:
Kosovo (there is no ISO 3166-1 alpha-3 code; KOS used here from United Nations Development Programme AND International Olympic Committee code) – declared independence in 2008; currently recognized by 113 UN members; NOT recognized by Serbia, China, Russia, Romania, Slovakia, Greece, Brazil.
Taiwan or Republic of China (TWN) – constitutionally formed in 1912; de jure part of the People's Republic of China; currently recognized as a state by 16 UN members and the Holy See.
Western Sahara or Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (ESH) – declared independence in 1976; currently recognized by 46 UN member states and South Ossetia. 38 other UN member states have recognized the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic but subsequently retracted or suspended recognition, pending the outcome of a referendum on self-determination. It does not make difference for Opiliones, because there are no records due to the desertic physiognomy.

4) Three states that are neither UN members nor UN observers and have only scarce recognition are NOT accepted in HarvEx and WCO:
Abkhazia – declared its independence in 1999; currently recognized by 5 UN member states and three UN non-member states. For the purposes of HarvEx and WCO its territory is included in Georgia.
Northern Cyprus or Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus – declared its independence in 1983; recognized only by Turkey. For the purposes of HarvEx and WCO its territory is included in Cyprus.
South Ossetia – declared its independence in 1991; currently recognized by 5 UN member states and four UN non-member states. For the purposes of HarvEx and WCO its territory is included in Georgia.

5) States recognized only by other non-UN member states are NOT accepted in HarvEx and WCO:
Republic of Artsakh or Nagorno-Karabakh Republic – declared its independence in 1991. It is recognized by three UN non-members: Abkhazia, South Ossetia and Transnistria. For the purposes of HarvEx and WCO its territory is included in Azerbaijan.
Transnistria or Pridnestrovian Moldavian Republic – declared its independence in 1990. It is recognized by three UN non-members: Abkhazia, Republic of Artsakh (Nagorno-Karabakh) and South Ossetia. For the purposes of HarvEx and WCO its territory is included in Moldova.

The user starts by choosing among three modes, depending on the kind of query he has.
The initial screen has three mode buttons: hand, leaf, globe, which respectively lead the user to:
Diversity by area/authors Mode,
Taxon distribution Mode
and
Checklist Mode.

Hand Mode
Diversity by area/authors Mode (Hand) can answer many different kinds of query, for example:
How many species/ genera/etc. occur in a given realm/continent/country? Can I have a visual representation of the predominance of a taxon in certain areas? filtered by more diverse genera to decrease the noise generated by the countless monotypic Roewerian genera? What is the composition of the opilionofauna of such and such place?
Here, in the Figure 1 we answer the questions:
What is the composition of opilionofauna of the Afrotropics?
Can I pick only the thickest slices of the pie? Yes, by using the slider to filter only the most diverse countries.
Now we know that the 6 most diverse families in the Afrotropics sum 695 species, and that Assamiidae is the richest.

Hand Mode - the Roewerian Effect
The high percentage of monotypic genera in Opiliones is a consequence of the systematic methods of the German author Carl-Friedrich Roewer, and it causes a disproportionate number of subdivisions which conceal any useful pattern. To deal with this effect, besides the slider which allows a cutoff line, the genera below a certain threshold are grouped into the "others" category in the pie chart and also nor depicted in the list to the right side. Otherwise the amount of super small slice of the pie would render it unreadable. This effect of course is maximized when dealing with very large areas and subdivided be genera instead of families for example.
The example below shows the Neotropical Realm without any filters and only with the genera which have more than a certain number of species individualized.

Leaf Mode
With HarvEx, students will be able to see the composition of families, subfamilies or genera by either realm or country, including filter for period of time and diversity of each genus.
Here in the example we may see the distributional range of the small family Kimulidae in six Neotropical countries.
